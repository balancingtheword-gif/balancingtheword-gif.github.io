A Growing Trend in Modern Families

Not a New Problem, But a New Trend

Family conflict is not a modern phenomenon. Throughout history, families have experienced disagreements, broken relationships, and periods of separation. Human nature has always created tension within the family unit.

What is new, however, is the growing frequency and normalization of family estrangement. Researchers, family therapists, and sociologists have observed a significant rise in adult children choosing to limit or completely sever contact with parents and other family members. What was once considered rare is now openly discussed in mainstream culture and across social media.

Family issues that were once kept private are now frequently shared in public forums and online communities. As a result, estrangement is increasingly presented as a legitimate solution to difficult family relationships rather than a tragic last resort.

Joshua Coleman argues that a major cultural shift over the past decade has fundamentally changed how people view family obligations, personal identity, emotional well-being, and relational boundaries. These changes have contributed to the growing prevalence of family estrangement.

Understanding this shift is essential. Before addressing family estrangement from a biblical perspective, it is important to understand what is happening and why so many families are being affected by it today.

The Silent Epidemic

Family estrangement has become so widespread that some researchers describe it as a "silent epidemic." While broken family relationships often remain hidden from public view, millions of parents, children, siblings, and extended family members are living with the pain of separation.

A YouGov poll of 4,395 adults found that four in ten respondents reported being estranged from at least one immediate family member, suggesting that estrangement is no longer a rare experience but a growing reality across many communities and generations.

Research from Ohio State University further illustrates the scope of the issue. The study found that approximately 6 percent of adult children are estranged from their mothers and 26 percent are estranged from their fathers, affecting millions of families across the United States.

The impact extends far beyond the individuals directly involved. Parents often experience grief and confusion, while adult children may struggle with rejection, disappointment, or unresolved hurt. Grandparents, siblings, and extended family members are frequently affected as well.

Researchers are paying close attention because strong family relationships have historically provided emotional support, caregiving, and stability. When those connections are broken, both generations may lose important sources of support and guidance.

As estrangement becomes increasingly common, researchers have begun examining the cultural and social factors that may be contributing to its rise.

Why Is This Happening?

While no single factor can fully explain the rise in family estrangement, Joshua Coleman points to several cultural changes that have reshaped how people view family relationships. Together, these shifts have created an environment in which estrangement has become increasingly common.

Among the factors Coleman identifies are divorce, political polarization, rising individualism, greater emphasis on mental health, the growth of online communities, and the influence of social media. Family restructuring and unresolved conflict can leave lasting relational wounds. Deep disagreements over politics and personal values have strained many family relationships. At the same time, many people now evaluate relationships through the lens of personal fulfillment, emotional well-being, and individual growth.

The internet and social media have further transformed how people find support and community. Online platforms provide encouragement and validation that previous generations often sought within the family, while also shaping how individuals view relationships and conflict.

Taken together, these cultural shifts help explain why family estrangement has become more visible, more accepted, and more common than in previous generations.

The Generational Shift

One of the most significant factors contributing to family estrangement is a shift in how different generations view family relationships. While conflict between parents and children is not new, expectations surrounding those relationships have changed dramatically. What previous generations viewed as normal family obligations are increasingly being evaluated through a different set of values.

For much of modern history, family relationships were held together by duty, loyalty, and obligation. Family members were expected to remain connected despite disagreements, and preserving family unity was often viewed as a responsibility that transcended personal preferences.

This approach was not without its flaws. Some individuals remained in unhealthy family situations because loyalty to family was often prioritized above personal well-being.

Today, many younger adults place greater emphasis on personal growth, emotional safety, and individual happiness. Relationships are often evaluated based on their impact on emotional well-being, leading some to create distance from relationships they perceive as harmful.

This shift helps explain why family conflicts are often interpreted differently by parents and adult children. Older generations may view family relationships as commitments that should be preserved despite disagreements, while younger generations may view them as dependent upon mutual respect, emotional support, and healthy interaction.

As a result, what one generation sees as loyalty, another may view as unhealthy obligation. What one generation considers healthy boundaries, another may perceive as rejection. These differing expectations help explain why family estrangement has become more common in recent years.

Concept Creep and Safetyism

Some mental health professionals have identified another cultural shift that may be influencing how people view family relationships. Licensed Marriage and Family Therapist Rachel Haack points to two related concepts known as "concept creep" and "safetyism." These ideas help explain why ordinary family tensions are increasingly being viewed through a therapeutic lens.

One aspect of this shift is the expansion of clinical terminology into everyday conversation. Terms such as trauma, abuse, narcissism, gaslighting, and boundaries have become part of the common vocabulary. While these terms describe real and often serious issues, some therapists caution that they are increasingly being applied to situations that may not meet their original clinical definitions.

Haack describes "concept creep" as the gradual expansion of concepts such as harm, trauma, and abuse to include experiences that previous generations may have considered ordinary disappointments, disagreements, or interpersonal conflicts. As definitions broaden, situations once viewed as normal relational struggles may increasingly be interpreted as emotionally damaging or unsafe.

Closely related is "safetyism," the idea that safety includes not only protection from physical harm but also protection from emotional discomfort and distress. Under this framework, a relationship may be viewed as unsafe because it causes stress, anxiety, or emotional pain, even when no abuse is present.

Critics argue that normal family tensions—including personality differences, misunderstandings, and recurring conflicts—are increasingly being categorized as harmful. As a result, some individuals may view distancing themselves from family members as the healthiest solution rather than pursuing resolution.

Historically, family estrangement most often occurred in cases involving severe abuse, abandonment, or addiction. Today, researchers report that estrangement may also occur because of emotional invalidation, lack of support, political differences, or perceived boundary violations. While these concerns should not be dismissed, they reflect a broader shift in how family relationships are evaluated.

At the same time, genuine trauma, abuse, and harmful behavior do exist. The concern raised by some therapists is not that these terms are invalid, but that their increasing use may blur important distinctions between severe harm and ordinary relational conflict.

Social Media and the No-Contact Movement

Social media has played a significant role in bringing family estrangement into public view. Platforms such as TikTok and Instagram contain thousands of posts associated with hashtags like #NoContact and #ToxicParents, creating communities where individuals share their experiences, seek advice, and find support. For many, these spaces provide validation and encouragement that may be difficult to find elsewhere.

Supporters argue that these communities can be especially helpful for individuals who have experienced genuine abuse, neglect, or other harmful family dynamics. By sharing their stories, many find reassurance that they are not alone and gain the courage to seek help or establish healthier boundaries.

At the same time, some researchers and therapists have expressed concern about what they describe as a form of "social contagion." They argue that social media can sometimes present estrangement as a quick solution to difficult family relationships, reducing complex situations to simple narratives and portraying separation as the preferred response to conflict.

Critics worry that normal disagreements, personality differences, and long-standing family conflicts may increasingly be viewed through the same lens as genuinely abusive situations. While every family situation is unique, concerns remain that social media can amplify one-sided perspectives and discourage efforts toward communication and reconciliation.

Social media is neither entirely beneficial nor entirely harmful in this discussion. It has provided support for many individuals while also shaping how people understand family relationships, boundaries, and estrangement. What is clear is that it has become one of the most influential forces in the modern conversation about family estrangement.

As these conversations continue to unfold, researchers have discovered another important reality: parents and adult children often have very different explanations for why estrangement occurs in the first place.

Different Realities

One of the most challenging aspects of family estrangement is that parents and adult children often view the same relationship through very different lenses. Family therapists have found that both sides frequently have different explanations for why the estrangement occurred, making reconciliation difficult because each person is operating from a different understanding of the situation.

Many adult children describe estrangement as a painful last resort after years of attempting to communicate concerns, express feelings, or establish boundaries. From their perspective, distancing themselves from a family member is often viewed as an act of self-protection rather than a rejection of family.

Parents, however, often tell a very different story. Many report being shocked when contact ends, leaving them confused, heartbroken, and searching for answers. They may struggle to understand what happened or wonder whether they overlooked problems that had been developing for years.

Generational differences may further complicate these perceptions. Older generations often emphasized authority, duty, and respect, while younger generations tend to place greater importance on emotional validation and personal boundaries. As a result, the same relationship may be interpreted very differently by each side.

This does not mean that one side is always right and the other is always wrong. Rather, these differing perspectives reveal that family estrangement is often more complex than it first appears. Understanding both experiences can foster greater compassion and provide a more complete picture of why so many families are struggling today.

Hope for Broken Families

Despite the growing prevalence of family estrangement, there is reason for hope. Research from Ohio State University found that approximately 80 percent of estranged adult children eventually reconcile with their mothers and roughly 70 percent reconcile with their fathers. While estrangement can be deeply painful, it is often not permanent.

Researchers note that reconciliation does not necessarily mean returning to the past. Restored relationships often involve new expectations, clearer boundaries, improved communication, and a greater understanding of one another's perspectives.

Behind every statistic is a story of real people navigating disappointment, misunderstanding, hurt, and loss. Understanding the cultural trends and social influences surrounding family estrangement helps explain why so many families are facing this challenge today. Yet understanding the trend is only the beginning.

While researchers can help us understand what is happening, they cannot answer the deeper questions surrounding forgiveness, reconciliation, boundaries, and healing. What guidance does Scripture provide for navigating broken family relationships?
