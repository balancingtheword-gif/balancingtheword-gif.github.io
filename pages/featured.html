Not My Will, but Yours

The Power of Prayer Through Jesus

A Question Many Wrestle With

Recently, I read an article about a pastor and his teenage son who were doubting the power of prayer. The son asked his father, “If there is power in prayer, then why is it that some people with ailments do not get healed?” He used a congregant who had cancer who asked for prayers for healing, but they died anyway. So, if there is power in prayer, why did this person die?

The father did not have a clear answer. Instead, he gave a common response: “Well, God moves in mysterious ways.” That was not enough.

Later, he discovered that his son had been keeping a record of prayers—who they were for, what was requested, and whether those prayers were answered. This deeply concerned him. Over time, he realized his son was no longer praying with the family. It seemed he had concluded that prayer was useless.

That realization stirred something within the father. Concern became conviction, and conviction led to action. No longer satisfied with surface-level answers, he set out to understand the truth about prayer—not only for his son, but for his own faith as well.

How Do We Define the Power of Prayer?

Many believers claim there is power in prayer—but how do they know? Is it only because some prayers appear to be answered? What about the ones that are not? Do they lack power?

Too often, unanswered prayers are explained as God simply saying “no.” However, did He truly say no—or is He teaching, shaping, or accomplishing something beyond what we can see?

These questions are not new—they are lived out in the everyday experiences of believers, often without us even realizing it. I remember this clearly from my own life.

When Prayer Becomes Self-Focused

As a former athlete, before our basketball games, the coach would gather the team into a huddle for prayer. Usually, the prayer was simple: that we would win and be protected from injury. At times, we would pray for the other team as well—but not with the same sincerity, and certainly not for them to win.

Over time, I began to wonder: What if the other team was praying the same thing? How was God supposed to answer both requests? Of course, only one team could win. So what did that mean? Did the losing team receive a “no” from God?

There were even times when I played for schools that did not pray at all, yet the results were the same—one team won, and the other lost. It did not seem that prayer determined the outcome in the way we assumed.

Even more telling was what happened after the game. When we won, I do not remember bowing our heads in gratitude. When we lost, there was no prayer then either. Prayer was reserved only for the moment before the game—and only for what we wanted.

Looking back, it reveals something deeper.

If we are honest, it sounds a bit selfish.

Prayer: The Source, Not the Outcome

This raises a deeper question: What is the power of prayer, and why do believers claim that there is power in it? To answer this, we must look beyond our assumptions and observe how Jesus approached prayer.

In Luke 11:1, the disciples came to Jesus with a clear request: “Lord, teach us to pray.” They even referenced John, asking to be taught as his disciples had been. Yet Scripture does not record how John taught them. What it does record—and what matters most—is what they witnessed in Jesus.

These disciples walked with Him daily. They saw Him teach, heal, cast out demons, and perform miracles. However, they did not ask Him how to do any of those things.

They asked Him how to pray.

Why? Because they recognized something essential: everything Jesus did flowed from His relationship with the Father. Prayer was not separate from His power—it was the source of it. It was through prayer that He maintained a personal and intimate communion with the Most High.

In that realization, we begin to understand that the power of prayer is not found in the outcome—but in the relationship it sustains.

What the Disciples Saw in Jesus

To answer this, we must look at Jesus the same way the disciples did—not just listening to His words, but observing His life. They did not ask to be taught in theory because they had witnessed something in Him. So the question becomes: What did Jesus actually do when He prayed?

Jesus demonstrated an intentional separation. He withdrew from the crowds, from the demands of ministry, and even from His own disciples. In Luke 5:16, it tells us that He regularly slipped away into the wilderness to pray. This was not occasional—it was consistent.

Other passages reveal that He also went to the mountains. These locations are not incidental; they are deeply significant and reveal the nature of His prayer life.

The Mountain: A Place of Clarity and Preparation

Going to the mountains required effort. He had to leave the crowds behind, step away from the pressures surrounding Him, and choose solitude. Throughout Scripture, mountains are often places of divine encounter—Moses on Mount Sinai (Exodus 19) and Elijah on Mount Horeb (1 Kings 19). These were not places where God was physically closer, but places where distractions were removed, allowing for greater spiritual focus and clarity.

Jesus followed this same pattern. He went to the mountains before major moments, such as before choosing the twelve disciples (Luke 6:12) and before the Transfiguration (Luke 9:28). These were times of preparation, revelation, and alignment with the Father’s will.

The Wilderness: A Place of Surrender and Refinement

The wilderness was another place where Jesus went to pray. Likely, the wilderness of Judea, located east of Jerusalem toward the Dead Sea, stretched toward Jericho and formed part of the Judean Desert. This region was arid and barren, marked by rocky terrain, sharp elevation changes, extreme heat during the day, and cold at night, with little to no water. It was a place of complete isolation.

So when Jesus went into the wilderness, He sought a place of emptiness and quietness, but it was uncomfortable. In the wilderness, there are no provisions, no support, no control, so dependence upon God alone is a necessity for survival. It is a place of testing like Israel’s 40-year wandering and the temptations of Jesus (Matt. 4). Here, faith is strengthened, and motives are exposed. Yet Jesus came to this wild and barren place to refocus, reconnect with the Father, and realign Himself with the Father’s will. It was a time of spiritual renewal.

The Contrast: Where Strength Is Found

Upon the mountain, Jesus pursued clarity, revelation, and preparation. In the wilderness, He sought surrender, dependence, testing, and refinement. He knew where His strength came from.

Prayer as a Way of Life

This pattern was not limited to where Jesus prayed—it was also revealed in when He prayed.

His prayer life was not occasional or situational. It was consistent, intentional, and woven into every part of His life. Whether in moments of preparation, pressure, decision, or completion, Jesus remained in continual communion with the Father.

To fully understand the power of prayer, we must also examine the timing of His prayer life.

Times That Jesus Prayed

Jesus prayed various times, but He was always in communion with His Father.

Early Morning Prayers

In Mark 1:35, it says He rose early in the morning, while it was still dark and before others were awake, to enter solitude before the day began. This reveals that prayer was His priority, not an afterthought. Also, He aligned Himself with the Father before His day began.

After a Hard Day of Work

After managing the demands of His ministry of teaching, healing, and feeding the crowds, He separated from the crowds for prayer, as referenced in Matthew 14:23 and Mark 6:46. In this manner, He did not allow for activity or busyness to replace intimacy with His Father. This was how He replenished and rested.

Dealing with Difficult Decisions

When He faced the important decision of choosing His twelve disciples, He spent the entire night in prayer. He sought the Father’s will and not human reasoning.

Before Important Events

He also prayed before and during significant events. For instance, His transfiguration (Luke 9:28) and before raising Lazarus from the dead (John 11:41–42). Therefore, before any pivotal spiritual moment, He prayed to be ready for revelation and power.

During Times of Distress and Pressure

He prayed during times of distress and pressure, such as His intense prayer before His captivity by the Roman soldiers (Luke 22:39–46). At this time, He was facing the cross; however, He needed to crucify His will—His flesh—through total surrender to the Father’s will before He could have the strength to be crucified. Ultimately, He submitted to the Father: “Not My will, but Yours.”

Thanksgiving and Praise

He prayed during moments of thanksgiving and praise (Matthew 11:25; John 6:11), openly acknowledging the Father and giving thanks not only for what was done, but for who God is. His prayers reflected trust, gratitude, and continual recognition of the Father’s provision.

Intercession for Others

He interceded for others (John 17), lifting up His disciples and all who would believe. His prayers were not centered on Himself, but on the spiritual strength, unity, and protection of others, revealing a heart aligned with the Father’s love and purpose.

Prayer on the Cross

On the cross, Jesus cried out in prayer (Matthew 27:46). Even in His final moments of suffering, His communion with the Father remained unbroken. In pain, in sacrifice, and in the fulfillment of His mission, He still turned to God.

The Pattern for Believers

As believers, Jesus is our example—not only in how to pray, but in when to pray. His life shows that prayer is not confined to specific moments, but woven into every part of life—before the day begins, after we have poured out, in times of decision and pressure, in gratitude, for others, and even at the end.

Prayer, then, is not an occasional act, but the continual rhythm of a life surrendered to God.

The Answer Found in Surrender

The father set out to find an answer—something that could explain prayer and restore his son’s faith. Like many of us, he was searching for clarity in outcomes.

Yet, the answer was not found in the results.

It was found in surrender.

What the disciples saw in Jesus, and what was revealed in Gethsemane, is this: the power of prayer is not in getting what we want, but in yielding to the will of the Father.

“Not My will, but Yours.”

This is where true strength is found.

The answer was never a formula or an explanation—it was a posture.

The power of prayer is this: Not my will, but Yours.
