Sanctification: A Call to Be Set Apart

A Call to Live Holy Before God

Introduction

Since the Easter celebration has recently passed, I have decided that this is an appropriate time to present a message on sanctification...

In my own walk with the Lord since 1986, I have heard very little thorough teaching on sanctification from behind the pulpit. More often than not, it is mentioned briefly, usually framed as a blessing, yet rarely explained in a way that brings clarity or direction. As a result, many important questions remain unanswered.

What is sanctification?

How do we know if we are sanctified?

Is there something required of us in this process?

How does sanctification shape our daily lives—or does it at all?

These are not small questions. They go to the heart of what it means to truly follow Christ. If we are called not only to believe in Him but also to live transformed lives, then understanding sanctification is not optional—it is essential.

Sanctify Christ as Lord

The apostle Peter writes to believers who were not living in comfort but under increasing pressure and persecution throughout Asia Minor. In the midst of that environment, he gives a striking instruction:

“Sanctify Christ as Lord in your hearts…” (1 Peter 3:15)

At first glance, this command may seem simple, yet it carries profound weight. What does it truly mean to “sanctify Christ,” and why would Peter emphasize this to believers who are already suffering?

To sanctify means “to set apart,” “to consecrate,” or “to regard as holy.” Peter is not suggesting that Christ becomes holier through our actions—He is already perfectly holy. Rather, the command is directed toward the believer: Christ is to be set apart in the heart as Lord above all. He is to be honored, revered, and given the highest place of authority within.

Sanctification Rooted in Israel

To understand what it means to sanctify Christ as Lord in our hearts, we must first understand sanctification itself. This concept is not new to the New Testament—it is deeply rooted in God’s relationship with Israel.

Sanctification is the process of being set apart for God’s purposes and being made holy. From the beginning, God called Israel to be a distinct people, revealing both His sovereign choice and their responsibility to live according to His statutes.

This begins with God’s covenant with Abraham, where He promises to make his descendants a great nation. It is then established more fully at Mount Sinai, where Israel is called to be a treasured possession, a kingdom of priests, and a holy nation. Here, sanctification is both God’s act of setting them apart and their call to obedience.

The Levitical laws reinforce this by emphasizing holiness in both worship and daily life, showing that their sanctification is rooted in God’s own holiness.

Yet Israel failed to live up to this calling. The prophets, however, pointed forward to a future work of God—one in which His people would be truly sanctified, not only outwardly but inwardly, as His law would be written on their hearts.

A Life Set Apart Daily

Building on this foundation, Israel’s sanctification was not merely a theological idea—it was lived out through tangible practices woven into their daily lives. God gave them specific instructions that illustrated what it meant to be set apart.

These practices often began with outward acts such as washing and purification, which symbolized the deeper need for inner cleansing before approaching God. Sanctification also required intentional separation—from sin, impurity, and anything that would compromise their identity as God’s people. At the same time, it was not limited to ritual. True sanctification was tied directly to obedience, as Israel was called to live according to God’s commands in every aspect of life.

Central to this process was the sacrificial system, where atonement was made through offerings, reminding them that sin had to be dealt with in order to maintain fellowship with God. In addition, people, objects, and even time were set apart for God’s exclusive use, reinforcing that sanctification touched every part of life—not just moments of worship.

Finally, sanctification was often corporate. The people were called to prepare themselves together through repentance and consecration, recognizing that they stood before God not only as individuals but as a covenant community.

In all of this, one truth becomes clear: sanctification in Israel was both external and internal, requiring action while pointing to a deeper need for transformation within.

When we bring these elements together, we see that sanctification in Israel was not divided between religious practice and daily living—it was one and the same. Every aspect of life became an expression of being set apart for God.

Israel lived by God’s Word, allowing His commands to shape their actions, decisions, and relationships. They were called to remember Him continually, keeping His presence at the center of their thoughts and routines. Even in the ordinary acts of eating and dressing, they were distinguished from the surrounding nations, as their dietary choices and clothing reflected their unique identity as God’s people.

This daily life was marked by a commitment to purity, both outward and inward, and by the observance of sacred times that regularly drew their attention back to God. Their worship was not occasional, but ongoing—seen in daily sacrifices, prayers, and blessings that acknowledged their dependence on Him.

At the same time, sanctification was evident in how they lived among others. They were called to act justly, to uphold righteousness, and to reject the practices of pagan cultures around them. In doing so, their lives became a visible testimony of God’s holiness.

In all of this, sanctification was not confined to ritual, nor reduced to personal devotion alone. It was a complete way of life—where worship, obedience, and daily living were inseparably joined.

From External Practice to Internal Transformation

While Israel’s sanctification was expressed through visible practices—what they ate, wore, and how they lived—these outward distinctions pointed to a deeper need that had not yet been fully realized. Though they were set apart as a nation, the prophets made it clear that true sanctification required more than external obedience. It required a transformed heart.

This is where the New Testament brings clarity and fulfillment.

Through Jesus Christ, sanctification is no longer centered on external regulations, but on an inward work of God within the believer.

The Responsibility & Cost of the Believer

Yet this does not remove responsibility—it defines it. Believers are called to actively participate in this work by presenting their bodies as a living sacrifice and renewing their minds. This involves turning away from sin, walking in obedience, and living in a manner worthy of the Lord.

Sanctification also becomes visible in how we conduct ourselves—through holy speech and behavior, love for others, and a commitment to moral purity.

Although sanctification is a blessing freely given at the moment of salvation, it must be cultivated, nurtured, and intentionally pursued as we grow in Christ.

When the Sacred Is Mixed with the Common

Modern Easter celebrations often mix cultural traditions with the resurrection of Christ. These elements are not rooted in Scripture and can distract from the truth.

I witnessed this firsthand. After attending a powerful Easter Sunday production presented by an internationally known church, I left deeply moved by the message of Christ’s sacrifice and resurrection. Yet as I walked out, I was immediately met with a person dressed in a bunny costume, greeting children who were excitedly holding Easter egg coloring books. In that moment, the focus shifted—from the cross to something entirely unrelated.

Throughout that same week, I also observed crosses among the Easter eggs in the church itself. What represents the suffering, death, and victory of Christ was placed alongside symbols that carry no connection to that reality.

Discernment, Compromise, and the Next Generation

The result is a blurred distinction between what is holy and what is common.

This is not merely about tradition—it is about perception and priority. When the sacred is presented alongside the ordinary without distinction, its meaning is diminished. What should produce reverence becomes familiar, and what should remain set apart becomes blended into culture.

The church has become tolerant of practices that obscure what should be holy.

A Call to Return

This same compromise is evident in how believers dress and conduct themselves among unbelievers. In seeking to fit into the world, many have lost their sense of righteousness. The result is confusion—especially among young people searching for something real, stable, and unchanging.

The call is clear: return. Repent. Live sanctified lives.

Set aside what is cultural when it interferes with what is holy.

We are called to sanctify Christ as Lord in our hearts. And this raises an important question: why would Peter emphasize this to believers who are already suffering? Because even in suffering, their lives were a testimony. As they endured hardship, the world would take notice and ask what sustained them. For this reason, they were to be ready to give an answer for the hope within them—yet with gentleness and reverence. Their holiness was not only for obedience, but for witness.

Examine yourself honestly: where has compromise crept in? Where has the line between what is holy and what is common been blurred? And we must also hold our churches accountable to uphold what is holy before God.

The call remains: repent, renew the mind, and return.

Not a faith that conforms—but one that stands firm.

Not a life that blends in—but one that is set apart.

For sanctification is not merely something we receive—it is something we live.

A Prayer for Sanctification

Heavenly Father,

We come before You with humility and reverence. Search our hearts and reveal where we have allowed compromise, where we have treated what is holy as common, and where we have fallen short of Your righteousness.

Forgive us, Lord, and lead us to true repentance. Renew our minds, purify our hearts, and teach us to walk in obedience. Help us to sanctify Christ as Lord within us, that our lives may reflect Your holiness in all we do.

Give us discernment to distinguish truth from tradition, courage to stand apart, and grace to live in a way that honors You. Let our words, actions, and conduct bear witness to the hope that is in us.

We ask this in the name of Jesus Christ, Amen.
